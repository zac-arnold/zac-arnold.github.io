Tell your non-tech friend a story about a time you got blocked on a simple problem

I hit a roadblock in the Built In Method - Capitalise Function challenge. I had a lot of trouble writing the code and I threw as many problem solving techniques as I could at it. I started with pseudocode, but soon found I couldn't work out how to use the .map object. I googled extensively and watched youtube tutorials but I couldn't get my head around the syntax. I checked the Sample Answer that was provided hoping I could work backwards but it was too dense for me to understand. After more googling I checked in with my facilitator who walked me through the task, and had me verbalise what was going on in his answer (like a in-person take on the Rubber Ducky Method). I was able to go back to my repl, and correctly use the right syntax, and solve the problem. 

I ran the gamut of emotions throughout this process . At the beginning I was eager to work it out. That was followed by a big helping of frustration and finally relief once I had come to an understanding. 

The majority of code that I was using in the challenge was new to me. Know I have a better understanding of mapping, splitting and joining strings and arrays, as well as the  toUpperCase() + .subsstr(1) and toString commands. 

Tell your non-tech friend a story about a time you got solved a problem in an elegant way.

I needed to write a set of code to find the multiples of 3, 5 and 15 and assign those multiples different words (Fizz, Buzz or Fizzbuzz). Using an if/else statement I was able to break this down so it was clear and easy to understand. Writing pseudocode has become my default when I begin working. This lead me to write myself clear instructions and create logical code. It was a confidence boost to breakdown half of the challenge quickly, and made me realise that writing and vocalising at the start of the problem solving process help you to gain clarity.
